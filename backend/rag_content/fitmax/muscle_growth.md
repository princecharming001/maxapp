# Muscle Growth Framework

Muscle growth has a small set of inputs that actually drive the result: mechanical tension, progressive overload, proximity to failure, and recovery. Soreness is feedback, not a goal.

## Mechanical Tension

The primary driver of hypertrophy. Your muscle has to produce force under resistance, especially in the stretched position, with controlled tempo.

What this means in practice:
- Don't bounce reps. The bottom should be controlled, not collapsed.
- Get a real stretch in the lengthened position (more growth signal).
- Squeeze the contraction; don't just move weight from A to B.

## Progressive Overload

You must increase demands over time. Options:

- Add weight (the obvious one)
- Add reps at the same weight
- Add a set
- Tighten form so the target muscle does more of the work
- Reduce rest so you do more in less time

You don't have to add weight every session. You do have to be progressing on at least one variable across a 4–8 week block. If nothing is going up, nothing is growing.

## Proximity To Failure

Most natural lifters undertrain.

- Working sets should leave 0–3 reps in reserve (RIR)
- The last 5 reps of a hard set drive most of the growth signal
- Sets stopped 5+ reps short of failure are mostly junk volume

Form should hold all the way to the last rep. If form breaks down 4 reps from failure, drop the weight and rebuild the rep range.

## Soreness Is Not Growth

Muscle damage is not the main growth signal. You can grow without ever being sore. You can be wrecked sore from a bad workout that didn't grow anything.

Use soreness as info ("was that volume tolerable? did I progress next session?") — not as a target.

## Frequency

Each muscle responds to being trained 1–3 times per week:

- 1×/week: works for advanced lifters with very high per-session volume; wastes recovery for most
- 2×/week: the sweet spot for most people
- 3×/week: useful for lagging muscles or skill-heavy lifts (squat, bench)

## Key Numbers

- **Natural muscle gain rate**: ~0.25–0.5 lb/week (0.5–2 lb/month) for intermediate lifters; beginners can hit 1–2 lb/month in the first year
- **Muscle protein synthesis window**: elevated for ~24–48 h post-training; peaks ~4–6 h after a session
- **Protein per meal**: ~25–40 g of high-quality protein maximizes per-meal MPS (leucine threshold ~2–3 g)
- **Weekly volume sweet spot**: 10–20 hard sets per muscle group; growth drops off past ~20 sets for most people
- **Strength progress rate**: ~1–3% per week early training; ~0.5–1% per week intermediate; plateaus are normal after ~2 years
- **Overtraining signal**: strength dropping on a key lift for 2+ consecutive sessions despite adequate sleep and food

## Recovery Inputs

You can't grow what you can't recover.

- Sleep 7–9 h consistently
- Hit your protein target (0.8–1 g/lb)
- Eat enough total calories — chronic deficit + heavy lifting = stalled growth
- Manage stress; cortisol blunts growth signals
- Take 1–2 full rest days per week

## When You're Not Growing

Audit in this order:

1. Are you progressing weight or reps over 4 weeks? (No → tighten form, then add a set.)
2. Are you eating enough? (Bodyweight should creep up if growing.)
3. Are you sleeping enough? (Track for two weeks honestly.)
4. Are you actually training to ~2 RIR or less? (Most people aren't.)
5. Are you doing too much junk volume? (More sets ≠ more growth past ~16/muscle.)
